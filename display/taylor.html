Taylor Series

Examples from Stewart

  1. Consider the exponential function \(\mathrm{e}^x\).
    1. Calculate the MacLaurin series for the exponential function.
    2. Evaluating this series at \(x = 1\) notice we now have a novel series with value \(\mathrm{e}\).
  2. Recall that we already know the series presentation \[\frac{1}{1-x} = 1+ x+x^2+x^3+\dotsb\] for \(|x| \lt 1\). Verify that this series is, in fact, the Taylor series for the function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}\) centered at \(x=0\).
  3. Find the MacLaurin series for sine and cosine and their respective radii of convergence.
  4. Find Maclaurin series for each of the following functions: \[ x\cos(x) \qquad \qquad \qquad \ln(1+3x^2) \]
  5. Calculate the sum of this series by recognizing it as the Taylor series of a familiar function. \[ \frac{1}{1\cdot 2^1} - \frac{1}{2\cdot 2^2} + \frac{1}{3\cdot 2^3} - \frac{1}{4\cdot 2^4} + \frac{1}{5\cdot 2^5} - \dotsb \]
  6. We've seen that there is no formula for the function \[ f(x) = \int\limits_0^x \mathrm{e}^{-t^2}\,\mathrm{d}t\,, \] but as luck would have it, there is a power series presentation of \(f\). Calculate the Taylor series for \(f\).
  7. Evaluate this limit by considering the Taylor series for \(\mathrm{e}^x\). \[\lim_{n \to 0} \frac{\mathrm{e}^x -1-x}{x^2} \]
  8. Find the first three non-zero terms of the Taylor series for each of the following functions. \[ \mathrm{e}^x\sin(x) \qquad \qquad \qquad \tan(x) \]

Challenges

  1. (Stewart) Consider the function \[ f(x) = \frac{x}{1-x-x^2}\,.\]
    1. Show that the Maclaurin series of the function is \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty F_nx^n\) where \(F_n\) is the \(n\)th Fibonacci number. Ie, \(F_1 = F_2 = 1\) and \(F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}\).
    2. By writing \(f(x)\) as a sum of partial fractions and thereby obtaining the Maclaurin series in a different way, find an explicit formula for the \(n\)th Fibonacci number.

    1. Show that for \(xy \neq -1\) \[\arctan(x) - \arctan(y) = \arctan\left(\frac{x-y}{1+xy}\right)\] if the left side lies between \(-\pi/2\) and \(\pi/2\).
    2. Show that \[\arctan\frac{120}{119} - \arctan\frac{1}{239} = \frac \pi 4\,.\]
    3. Deduce the following formula of John Machin (1680-1751): \[4\arctan\frac 1 5 - \arctan\frac{1}{239} = \frac{\pi}{4}\]
    4. Use the Maclaurin series for arctan to show that \[ 0.197395560 \lt \arctan\frac 1 5 \lt 0.197395562 \] and \[ 0.004184075 \lt \arctan\frac{1}{239} \lt 0.004184077 \]
    5. Deduce that, correct to seven decimal places, \(\pi \approx~ 3.1415927\)
    Machin used this method in 1706 to find \(\pi\) correct to 100 decimal places. Recently, with the aid of computers, the value of \(\pi\) has been computed to increasingly greater accuracy. In 1999, Takahashi and Kanada, using methods of Borwein and Brent/Salamin, calculated the value of \(\pi\) to 206,158,430,000 decimal places.
