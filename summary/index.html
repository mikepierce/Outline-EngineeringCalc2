Sometimes the purpose of taking a course becomes obfuscated over its duration; it can become hard to appreciate the plot of a story when there’s too much exposition. This narrative overview, in its brevity, serves to summarize the plot of this course and to clarify our purpose, for the sake of keeping both student and instructor focused on what’s important.

Transcendental Functions

In this late segment of the course, we finally come around to introducing the transcendental functions \(\ln(x)\) and \(\mathrm{e}^x\).

Although it should be pointed out, the trigonometric functions sine, tangent, secant, etc, are also trigonometric functions; the designers of most textbooks subtitled “late transcendentals” don’t appear to be bothered by this.

We go about introducing these functions by defining the (natural) logarithmic function in terms of an integral \[ \ln(x) = \int\limits_1^x \frac{1}{t}\,\mathrm{d}t\,, \] and from there defining \(\mathrm{e}^x\) as \(\ln^{-1}(x)\) and investigating their derivatives, their antiderivatives, and exponential/logarithmic functions of an arbitrary base from there.

  • Skill

    Be able to calculate the derivative of any rational sum, product, or composite of exponential, logarithmic, or trigonometric functions.

  • Skill

    Be able to calculate the integral of any rational sum of exponential or logarithmic functions, or any integral that yields to a simple substitution.

  • Skill

    Be familiar with the technique of logarithmic differentiation, and know that this technique is critically helpful for calculating the derivatives of functions of the form \(f(x)^{g(x)}\) for non-constant \(f\) and \(g\).

TK

  • Concept

    Know that we can express the derivative of the inverse of a function in terms of the derivative of that function. In particular, know that we can find algebraic formulas for the derivatives of the inverse trigonometric functions. This idea is known as the inverse function theorem.
