Sometimes the purpose of a course becomes obfuscated over its duration; it can become hard to appreciate the overarching plot of a story when one spends months buried in fine exposition and occasional side-plots. This narrative overview, in its brevity, serves to summarize the plot of this course and to clarify our purpose, for the sake of keeping both student and instructor focused on what’s important.

Transcendental Functions

In this second semester of calculus we finally come around to introducing the transcendental functions \(\ln(x)\) and \(\mathrm{e}^x\).

Although it should be pointed out, the trigonometric functions sine, tangent, secant, etc, are also transcendental functions; the designers of most calculus textbooks subtitled “late transcendentals” don’t appear to be bothered by this.

We introduce these functions by defining the (natural) logarithm as a definite integral \[ \ln(x) = \int\limits_1^x \frac{1}{t}\,\mathrm{d}t\,, \] and from there defining \(\mathrm{e}^x\) as \(\ln^{-1}(x)\). After starting from this perspective though, it becomes pertinent to figure out how the derivative of the inverse of a function relates the function itself. Luckily the relationship is quite simple.

  • Concept

    Know that the derivative of the inverse of a function can be expressed in terms of the derivative of that function as \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{f'\left(f^{-1}(x)\right)}\,. \] This is known as the inverse function theorem.

We can get a lot of information from the inverse function theorem, not just about the derivative of \(\mathrm{e}^x\) and about exponential/logarithmic function of non-natural bases, but also about the inverses of trigonometric functions. This information adds to our toolbox of differentiation and integrations skills.

  • Skill

    Be able to calculate the derivative of any rational sum, product, or composite of exponential or logarithmic functions of any base, and of inverse trigonometric functions: arcsine, arctangent, arcsecant, etc.

  • Skill

    Be able to calculate the antiderivative of any rational sum of exponential functions, or any antiderivative involving an exponential that yields to substitution.

  • Skill

    Recognize the patterns of the derivatives of logarithmic and inverse trigonometric functions when they appear as integrands so as to calculate integrals when they appear.

Beyond that core thread of new skills, we explore two miscellaneous skills that required the language of logarithms to develop in their entirety.

  • Skill

    Be familiar with the technique of logarithmic differentiation, and know that this technique is essential to calculate the derivative of functions of the form \(f(x)^{g(x)}\) for non-constant \(f\) and \(g\). Explicitly as a formula, \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} \left(f(x)^{g(x)}\right) = f(x)^{g(x)}\left(g(x)\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + g'(x)\ln\big(f(x)\big) \right). \]

  • Skill

    Know how and when to apply L’Hospital’s Rule to investigate the value of a limit.

Before we conclude our exploration of transcendental functions and move on to a new chapter of the story, there is one last sight we really should see, just to say we saw it.

  • Concept

    Be familiar with hyperbolic trigonometric functions: what they are, how they relate to exponential functions, and what their derivatives and antiderivatives look like.

The addition of these logarithmic, exponential, trigonometric, and hyperbolic functions completes the dictionary of differentiable functions we’ve been building in this class, and so we are now well-prepared to dive into the art and practice of evaluating integrals.

Techniques & Applications of Integration

We’ve seen already that its more complicated to calculate antiderivative than derivatives. Now we dip our toes into this complication. Now begins the gauntlet.

We know already that integrals are linear: they break up across sums and constants factor out. We also know to recognize a few integrals on sight simply as being the derivatives of familiar functions. And we have a single tool for computing derivatives, substitution, which “undoes” the chain-rule of differentiation. The first new tool we’ll develop in this class is called integration by-parts, a way of “undoing” the product-rule of differentiation. The essence of integration by-parts is the formula, \[ \int u \,\mathrm{d}v = uv - \int v \,\mathrm{d}u\,, \]

With this tool we’ll finally be able to calculate formulas for the integrals \[ \int \ln(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \quad\text{and}\quad \int \sec(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \] and move on to learning the tricks for dealing with more complicated trigonometric integrands like these: \[ \int \sin^n(x)\cos^m(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \quad \int \sec^n(x)\tan^m(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \quad \int \sin(ax)\cos(bx)\,\mathrm{d}x \] The “trick” is to be able to fluently wield trigonometric identities to rewrite the integrands, particularly the Pythagorean identities \[ cos^2(x) + \sin^2(x) = 1 \qquad 1 + \tan^2(x) = sec^2(x) \] along with the double/half-angle formulas and the product-to-sum formulas. And this is a good trick; we get extra mileage out of those two Pythagorean identities due to their ability to turn a sum/difference of squares into a single square term, a technique known as trigonometric substitution.

The last technique of integration we’ll cover isn’t so much about integration as it is about writing a rational function as a sum of smaller rational functions. This sum is called its partial fraction decomposition. To take the integral of a rational functions it’s much easier to take the integral of these smaller summands individually. While learning these we see that it also pays be generally be adept at algebraic manipulations while integrating functions.

  • Skill

    Be able to employ any of the previous tools/techniques to calculate formulas for indefinite integrals.

This concludes the gauntlet of learning to manually compute some integrals, so we should check back in with reality. Not every integrals is computable. I.e. not every integral can be expressed as a formula in terms of elementary functions. In practice when we need to compute the value of a definite integral we can use a computer to approximate it to arbitrary precision. We’ve already talked about Riemann sums and the midpoint rule and the trapezoid rule, but there are other methods.

  • Concepts

    Be familiar with techniques of numerically computing definite integrals, particularly with the trapezoid rule and with Newton–Cotes formulas of higher degree, and the bounds on their error.

And in recent history there’s been advances in symbolically computing the formulas for indefinite integrals using sufficiently sophisticated symbolic computer algebra systems (CAS). You can get a small taste of this sophistication by asking WolframAlpha to evaluate an integral for you.

Before moving on to applications of integration we should address a geometric quirk that we’ve ignored so far, but will become crucial to think about later when discussing series. Unbounded regions may contain a finite amount of area. Considering unbounded regions “under” curves and the integrals that compute their area, this means our bounds of integration may be \(\pm\infty\), or that our curve may have an asymptote (pole) between its bounds of integration. Such integrals are called improper. How do we handle improper integrals computationally? With limits. If our bounds of integration are infinite, \[ \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x = \lim_{L\to-\infty}\int\limits_{L}^{0} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x + \lim_{R\to\infty}\int\limits_{0}^{R} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x \,, \] or if \(f\) has a pole at \(p\) between \(a\) and \(b\), \[ \int\limits_{a}^{b} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x = \lim_{\ell\to p}\int\limits_{a}^{\ell} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x + \lim_{\rho\to p}\int\limits_{\rho}^{b} f(x) \,\mathrm{d}x \,. \]

  • Skill

    Be able to navigate the nuances of definite integrals with bounds \(\pm \infty\), or that contain poles within their domain of integration, determining whether they converge or diverge, and computing their value if they converge.

After going over these techniques and becoming comfortable with improper integrals, we’ll talk about a few “applications” of integration. The first are applications within mathematics, specifically within geometry, whereas the others are sincere applications,

  • Skill

    Be able to write down an integral that computes the arclength of a planar curve, and be able to write down an integral that computes the surface area of a solid generated by revolving a planar region about an axis.

  • Skill

    Be able to write down integrals that compute the coordinates of the center of mass (centroid) of a given planar region.

  • Concept

    Be familiar with other applications of integration to biology, economics, and probability.
